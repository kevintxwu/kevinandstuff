Epic Everything
Collectible Card Game

I led a team of over 30 professionals in building and marketing a real-time collectible card game built for both mobile and desktop platforms. We raised over $40,000 dollars before deciding to open source the project.

View Trailer View Kickstarter View Site

For more about Epic Everything itself, please visit the Kickstarter campaign! I wrote it to focus on intriguing mechanics and show off beautiful assets.

The spark for Epic Everything started with a hackathon project. It was an incomplete iOS game that turned into something more. We continued working on it with Unity instead to create a more fully featured game.

I managed a global team, from local Berkeley developers, to marketing associates across North America, to concept artists from Russia. We ended up with not only a beautiful game, but a methodical distribution plan.

Using the power of social media, we accumulated a total of five thousand followers on Facebook and Twitter combined, while assigning each marketing associate to act as a brand representative in their local area.

Epic Everything is a storybook world with creatures of all shapes and sizes, ranging from heroic adventurers to misguided robots to mischievous spirits. There's a diverse cast of inspired creatures and characters waiting to be discovered. We created these characters to be comical and approachable, bursting with personality. This allowed us to target a wider, mainstream audience, despite our fantasy theme.

An interesting design challenge was the card interface. We wanted it to retain all the readability and simplicity of a well-designed card interface like those in Hearthstone or Magic The Gathering, but we had to distinguish our own style of cards somehow. Our early iterations looked like half-assed copies, but we later discovered how to differentiate ourselves: with vibrant colors and textures.

Approaching the end of our crowdfunding campaign, it was clear we would reach our funding goal, but only barely. As a result, we were faced with a tough decision. Would we make the game with barely enough funding to go by, risking a sub-par, meaningless product? Though we were unable to procure the funds to guarantee success, to act on our gratitude for the support from the community we open sourced what we had accomplished.
